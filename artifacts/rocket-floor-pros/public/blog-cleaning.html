How to Clean and Maintain Your Epoxy Garage Floor: A Complete Care Guide

By Rocket Floor Pros · January 2025 · 9 min read

One of the best things about a professionally installed epoxy or polyaspartic floor coating is how easy it is to maintain. Compare it to bare concrete — which absorbs every oil drip, collects dust in its pores, and stains permanently — and you'll understand why our customers consistently say cleaning time dropped by 80% after their installation.

But "easy to maintain" doesn't mean "no maintenance." This guide gives you the complete routine for keeping your coated floor looking showroom-fresh for 15, 20, or even 25+ years. We also cover what to avoid — some common cleaning products and habits that can dull or damage a perfectly good coating.

Understanding What Your Floor Is Made Of

Before we get into the cleaning specifics, it helps to understand what you're cleaning. A professionally installed garage floor coating typically consists of:

  • Concrete substrate: Your existing slab — properly prepared, ground, and repaired.
  • Base coat: An epoxy or polyaspartic layer that bonds to the concrete.
  • Decorative flake/chip layer: Color chips broadcast into the wet base coat for aesthetics and grip.
  • Clear topcoat: A UV-stable polyaspartic clear coat that protects everything underneath and gives the floor its gloss and durability.

When you clean your floor, you're primarily cleaning the topcoat — a very hard, chemical-resistant, non-porous surface. This is why spills don't stain and dirt doesn't embed the way it does on bare concrete. The topcoat is your friend, and the goal of your cleaning routine is to protect it.

Weekly Routine: Dust Mopping (5–10 Minutes)

This is the single most important thing you can do for your floor's longevity. Sand, grit, and abrasive particles tracked in from driveways and roads act like sandpaper under foot traffic and tires. Over time, they can micro-scratch and dull the topcoat's gloss.

What to Use

  • Microfiber dust mop: The best tool for epoxy floors. Picks up fine particles without scratching. A 24" or 36" head covers a 2-car garage in minutes.
  • Soft-bristle broom: Works fine, but a microfiber mop is more effective for fine particles.
  • Vacuum (soft brush attachment): Great for getting along walls and in corners.

What to Avoid

  • Stiff-bristle brooms: Can leave fine scratches over time — especially on high-gloss finishes.
  • Shop vacuums with hard plastic nozzles: Drag the nozzle edge across the floor and can scratch. Use soft attachments only.

A quick dust mop every week — even just focusing on the tire tracks and main foot traffic areas — keeps the abrasive load down and significantly extends the life of your topcoat's gloss.

Monthly Routine: Wet Mopping (15–20 Minutes)

Once a month (or after any significant mess), a wet mop with a diluted cleaner removes oils, residue, and any stuck-on particles that the dust mop missed.

The Right Cleaner

Use a pH-neutral, soap-free floor cleaner diluted in water. Our go-to recommendation for customers is Simple Green diluted about 1:10 in water — it's gentle, effective, and widely available. Zep Neutral pH Floor Cleaner is another excellent option.

You can also use plain warm water for routine mopping — it works better than you might expect on a non-porous polyaspartic topcoat.

What to Avoid

  • Soap-based cleaners (dish soap, Castile soap, Pine-Sol): These leave a soapy film residue that builds up over time, dulls the finish, and makes the floor slippery when wet. This is the most common mistake we see.
  • Bleach or ammonia-based cleaners: Can degrade the topcoat chemistry over time and yellow lighter colored coatings. Occasional very dilute use is tolerable but not recommended as a routine.
  • Citrus-based degreasers (concentrated): The d-limonene in citrus cleaners can attack some polyaspartic formulations over time. Diluted use is generally fine; don't let concentrated citrus cleaner sit on the floor.
  • Abrasive scrubbing pads: Steel wool, scotch-brite (non-soft), and hard scrub brushes will leave visible scratches on a high-gloss topcoat.

Mopping Technique

  1. Dust mop or sweep the floor first to remove loose debris.
  2. Fill a bucket with warm water and a small amount of pH-neutral cleaner.
  3. Wring the mop out well — you want the mop damp, not soaking wet.
  4. Mop in sections, working toward the garage door so you're not stepping back over wet areas.
  5. Allow the floor to air dry (it dries quickly given the non-porous surface) or give it a final pass with a dry microfiber mop.

Immediate Action: How to Handle Spills

The non-porous nature of your polyaspartic topcoat means most spills won't stain — as long as you clean them up within a reasonable time. Here's how to handle the most common garage spills:

Motor Oil and Automotive Fluids

Motor oil, transmission fluid, and brake fluid won't permanently stain a polyaspartic topcoat the way they would bare concrete. But don't let them sit for days either — they can attract dirt and make the surface slippery.

  1. Soak up the bulk of the spill with paper towels or a clean rag.
  2. Apply a small amount of Simple Green or diluted degreaser to the remaining residue.
  3. Let it sit for 2–3 minutes, then wipe up with a clean cloth.
  4. Rinse with clean water.

For old, dried-on oil residue that's been sitting for weeks or months: use a plastic scraper to remove the bulk, then treat with degreaser. Don't use metal scrapers — they can gouge the topcoat.

Gasoline

Gasoline is more aggressive than motor oil and should be cleaned up promptly. Wipe up immediately with absorbent rags, then clean the area with diluted Simple Green. Don't use solvent-based cleaners to clean up gasoline — you'll just spread the problem and risk damaging the topcoat.

Battery Acid

Battery acid (sulfuric acid) is one of the few substances that can permanently etch an epoxy or polyaspartic floor if left in contact. If you have a battery spill, dilute immediately with large amounts of water, then neutralize with a dilute baking soda solution, and mop clean. Don't let it sit.

Antifreeze

Antifreeze is visually obvious (usually green or orange) and moderately hazardous to pets. Soak it up immediately with paper towels and clean with diluted Simple Green. It's not particularly damaging to the topcoat but should be cleaned promptly for safety reasons.

Water and Road Salt in Winter

Huntsville winters are mild but can involve road salt from pre-treatment of bridges and roads. Salt accumulation can dull the surface over time. Make a habit of doing a wet mop with clean water in winter months after driving on salted roads — especially February through March.

Seasonal Deep Clean: Twice a Year

Twice a year — typically spring and fall — give your floor a more thorough cleaning:

  1. Remove everything from the floor — park cars outside, pull out any floor mats or storage.
  2. Blow out corners and edges — use a leaf blower or shop vac to get into every corner.
  3. Hose it down or use a garden hose — the floor can get completely wet without any damage. A garden hose on "shower" setting works well, or use a pressure washer on the lowest pressure setting (under 1,500 PSI; stay 12+ inches back from the surface).
  4. Apply diluted Simple Green to any problem areas — tire marks, fuel residue, or visible staining — and let it dwell for 5 minutes before rinsing.
  5. Squeegee toward the garage door to move the water out, then allow to air dry.

After this cleaning, take a look at the floor in good lighting. A well-maintained polyaspartic floor should still look nearly as glossy as the day it was installed. If you notice localized dull patches that don't respond to cleaning, call us — it may indicate a topcoat issue covered under your warranty.

What About Floor Mats?

Floor mats are a topic that comes up constantly. Here's our guidance:

Rubber-Backed Mats: Use with Caution

Rubber-backed mats can trap moisture and road chemicals between the mat and the floor. Over months or years, this can discolor the floor surface under the mat. If you use rubber-backed mats, move them regularly and clean underneath them. Don't leave them in place permanently.

Foam Anti-Fatigue Mats

Common in garage workshops — these are generally fine for epoxy floors. Just remove and clean underneath them periodically.

PVC Parking Mats

Clear or patterned PVC parking mats (the kind designed to go under cars) are compatible with epoxy and polyaspartic coatings. They protect the floor from tire marks and drips. Look for PVC mats specifically rated for "garage floor use" — some cheaper vinyl mats can leave marks if they sit in place in summer heat.

Absorbent Oil-Drip Mats

Drip mats under vehicles with known leaks are a good idea regardless of what kind of floor you have. They protect your investment. Just make sure the mat itself is rated for fuel and oil and won't bleed dye onto your coating.

Long-Term Protection: What Not to Do

Here's a list of the most common things that cause premature wear on coated floors:

  • Dragging heavy metal objects: Car jacks, toolboxes, and heavy steel casters dragged across the floor can gouge or deeply scratch the topcoat. Use rubber wheels, move on a dolly, or place a plywood sheet under heavy loads when repositioning.
  • Metal shop stools and chairs: Metal legs without rubber or felt tips will scratch. Add rubber feet to all metal-legged furniture in the garage.
  • Waxing or polishing the floor: Don't. Polyaspartic topcoats don't benefit from waxes or floor polishes — those are for wood and VCT tile. Applying wax creates a slippery surface and attracts more dirt.
  • Parking very hot vehicles immediately after driving: We mentioned hot tire pickup in our epoxy vs. polyaspartic guide. A properly installed polyaspartic system handles hot tires well — but if you install a budget epoxy system with a thin clear coat, avoid parking immediately after highway driving on very hot days.
  • Using acidic cleaners regularly: Vinegar and citrus-based cleaners used routinely can gradually etch and dull polyaspartic topcoats. Use pH-neutral cleaners.

When to Call Your Installer

A professionally installed floor with a warranty should last many years without needing intervention. But here are situations where you should reach out to us:

  • Bubbling or delamination: If any section of the coating begins to bubble up or peel away from the concrete, that's a warranty item — contact us immediately.
  • Visible scratches that don't clean out: Deep gouges from dropped tools or dragged metal can sometimes be spot-repaired. Call us for an evaluation.
  • Yellowing or discoloration: This shouldn't happen with a proper polyaspartic topcoat — UV-stable coatings resist yellowing. If you're seeing significant color change, it may indicate an issue with the topcoat material used.
  • Loss of gloss in sections: Localized dullness that doesn't respond to cleaning can indicate topcoat failure or damage. We'll assess it under warranty.

Final Thoughts: Protecting Your Investment

An epoxy or polyaspartic floor represents a meaningful investment in your home. The return — in function, aesthetics, resale value, and daily ease of use — is real. A simple maintenance routine extends that return indefinitely.

The customers we hear from years after installation always say the same two things: the floor still looks great, and they wish they'd done it sooner. A little weekly attention is all it takes.
